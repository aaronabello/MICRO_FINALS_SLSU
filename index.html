Chapter 9 Overview

Chapter 9 explains the hardware side of the 8086 and 8088 microprocessors. It covers pin functions, DC characteristics, clock generation, bus buffering, bus latching, timing, wait states, minimum mode, maximum mode, and the 8288 bus controller.

Before connecting memory, I/O, buffers, latches, or coprocessors to the 8086/8088, you must understand the pin functions and bus timing.

Study flow: Pins → DC characteristics → 8284A clock generator → demultiplexing/buffering → bus timing → READY/wait states → minimum/maximum mode → 8288 bus controller.

Chapter Objectives

  1. Describe each 8086 and 8088 pin.
  2. Understand DC characteristics and fan-out.
  3. Use the 8284A clock generator.
  4. Connect buffers and latches to buses.
  5. Interpret timing diagrams.
  6. Describe wait states and related circuitry.
  7. Differentiate minimum and maximum mode.

9-2 Clock Generator: 8284A

Figures 9-2 to 9-4 cover 8284A pin-out, internal block diagram, and connection to 8086/8088.

The 8284A provides clock generation, RESET synchronization, READY synchronization, and a TTL-level peripheral clock.

PinFunction
AEN1/AEN2Qualify RDY1/RDY2 respectively.
RDY1/RDY2Bus ready inputs used to cause wait states.
ASYNC1=one synchronization stage, 0=two stages.
READYOutput to CPU READY input.
X1/X2External crystal connections.
F/C0=crystal oscillator, 1=EFI external frequency.
EFIExternal frequency input.
CLKOutput to CPU; frequency ÷3; 33% duty.
PCLKPeripheral clock; frequency ÷6; 50% duty.
OSCTTL-level output at same frequency as crystal/EFI.
RESActive-low reset input.
RESETOutput to CPU RESET.
CSYNCClock synchronization; ground when crystal is used.
GND/VCCGround and +5 V ±10%.

Clock Section Operation

  1. Crystal on X1/X2 creates square wave.
  2. Signal goes to AND gate and inverting buffer.
  3. Buffer produces OSC.
  4. F/C=0 sends oscillator to divide-by-3 counter.
  5. F/C=1 sends EFI to the counter.
  6. Divide-by-3 creates CLK and timing for READY synchronization.
  7. Divide-by-3 plus divide-by-2 creates divide-by-6 PCLK.
OSC = crystal or EFI frequency
CLK = crystal or EFI ÷ 3
PCLK = crystal or EFI ÷ 6
CrystalOSCCLKPCLK
15 MHz15 MHz5 MHz2.5 MHz
14 MHz14 MHz4.67 MHz2.33 MHz

Reset Section

The reset section uses a Schmitt trigger buffer and D-type flip-flop. RESET is applied on the negative clock edge; CPU samples RESET on the positive clock edge. RC circuit gives power-on reset; pushbutton allows manual reset.

  • RESET must become logic 1 no later than four clocks after power is applied.
  • RESET must be held high at least 50 µs.
  • CPU starts at FFFF0H and clears IF.

9-3 Bus Buffering and Latching

Figures 9-5 to 9-8 show demultiplexed and fully buffered 8088/8086 systems.

The 8086/8088 buses are multiplexed to reduce pins. Memory and I/O require stable addresses, so demultiplexing is required.

BusPurpose
AddressMemory address or I/O port number
DataData transfer between CPU, memory, and I/O
ControlRead/write/control signals

Demultiplexing

  • 8088: uses two 74LS373/74LS573 latches for AD7–AD0 and A19/S6–A16/S3.
  • 8086: uses three 74LS373 latches for AD15–AD0, A19/S6–A16/S3, and BHE/S7.
  • ALE=1 makes transparent latch pass inputs; ALE=0 stores the address.
  • 8088 result: A19–A0 address bus and D7–D0 data bus.
  • 8086 result: A19–A0 plus BHE, D15–D0, and control bus M/IO, RD, WR.
  • 20 address bits allow 1 MB memory addressing.

Buffering

If more than 10 unit loads are connected to a bus pin, buffering is required. Buffers also drive high-capacitance buses.

Buffered OutputDrive
Logic 0Up to 32 mA sink current
Logic 1Up to 5.2 mA source current
Full buffering adds timing delay, important when memory/I/O operates near maximum bus speed.
SystemComponents
Fully buffered 8088Two 74LS244, one 74LS245, two 74LS373
Fully buffered 8086One 74LS244, two 74LS245, three 74LS373

74LS245 direction is controlled by DT/R and enabled by DEN.

9-4 Bus Timing

Figures 9-9 to 9-13 show write/read bus cycles, read timing, AC characteristics, and write timing.

Bus timing determines if memory and I/O devices are fast enough.

Read and Write

  • Write: CPU outputs address, outputs data, and activates WR.
  • Read: CPU outputs address, activates RD, and accepts data on data bus.

T States

StatePurpose
T1Address is sent; ALE, DT/R, IO/M or M/IO output.
T2RD or WR issued; DEN activated; READY sampled at end.
T3Memory access; read data sampled at end.
T4Signals deactivated; write completes at trailing WR edge.
TwWait state between T2 and T3.
One bus cycle = 4 T states
At 5 MHz: clock period = 200 ns, bus cycle = 800 ns
Transfer rate = 1 / 800 ns = 1.25 million transfers/sec

At 5 MHz, the internal queue allows about 2.5 MIPS in bursts. At 10 MHz, about 5 MIPS in bursts.

Memory Access Time

Access time = 600 ns - 110 ns - 30 ns = 460 ns

With decoder and buffer delay, practical memory should be about 420 ns or faster. RD strobe width is 325 ns at 5 MHz.

9-5 READY and the Wait State

Figures 9-14 to 9-18 show READY timing, RDY timing, 8284A synchronization, and wait-state generator.

READY inserts wait states for slow memory/I/O. A wait state, Tw, is inserted between T2 and T3.

At 5 MHz: one wait adds 200 ns
460 ns + 200 ns = 660 ns with one wait state
  • READY sampled at end of T2.
  • If READY=0, Tw is inserted.
  • READY sampled again in middle of Tw.
  • If READY becomes 1, cycle continues to T3.
  • If READY is grounded, waits continue.

8284A RDY Synchronization

  • RDY1 AND AEN1.
  • RDY2 AND AEN2.
  • Outputs are ORed, then synchronized.
  • AEN1 and RDY1 must both be logic 1 for that ready path.
  • ASYNC=1: one stage. ASYNC=0: two stages.

Wait-State Generator

A 74LS164 8-bit serial shift register can generate 0 to 7 wait states. QB gives one wait; QC gives two waits. It shifts first on the positive edge of T2 and only generates waits for selected memory needing waits.

9-6 Minimum Mode Versus Maximum Mode

Minimum Mode

Least expensive. CPU generates all memory/I/O control signals. Similar to Intel 8085A and allows 8085A 8-bit peripherals without special considerations.

Maximum Mode

Used with external coprocessors such as 8087. Some control signals must be generated externally by the 8288 bus controller. Maximum mode was dropped starting with 80286.

Quick Review

Chapter 9 Quick Summary

  1. 8086 has 16-bit data bus; 8088 has 8-bit data bus.
  2. Both require +5 V ±10%.
  3. Noise immunity is derated to 350 mV.
  4. Direct fan-out is limited to about 10 unit loads.
  5. 8284A provides clock generation, RESET synchronization, READY synchronization, and PCLK.
  6. 15 MHz crystal gives 5 MHz CLK and 2.5 MHz PCLK.
  7. Reset starts execution at FFFF0H and clears IF.
  8. Multiplexed buses must be demultiplexed using ALE-controlled latches.
  9. Large systems need buffers.
  10. One bus cycle has T1, T2, T3, T4.
  11. READY low inserts Tw between T2 and T3.
  12. Minimum mode is simple and cheaper; maximum mode is for coprocessors and uses 8288.
